Question

Which of the following statements is true?

Answer

This picture is unremarkable: two horizontal lines of different lengths, with fins appended, pointing in different directions.

The bottom line is obviously longer than the one above it.

That is what we all see, and we naturally believe what we see.

If you have already encountered this image, however, you recognise it as the famous Muller-Lyer illusion.

As you can easily confirm by measuring them with a ruler, the horizontal lines are in fact identical in length.

You now know the lines are equally long. If asked about their length, you will say what you know. But you still *see* the bottom line is longer. You have chosen to believe the measurement, but you cannot prevent your instincts from doing its thing; you cannot decide to see the lines as equal, although you know they are.

To resist the illusion, there is only one thing you can do: you must learn to mistrust your impressions of the length of the lines when fins are attached to them.

from Daniel Kahneman: 'Thinking Fast and Slow', pg 27
